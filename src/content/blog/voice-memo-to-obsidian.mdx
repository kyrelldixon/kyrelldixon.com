## I'm outside

The best ideas I've had in the last year have shown up on walks. Some came back home with me. Most didn't.

The pattern: I'd think something sharp, feel the click, tell myself I'd remember — and by the time I was at the laptop, there'd be a vague outline where the thought used to be. Sometimes not even that.

Typing faster on my phone wasn't the fix. Thumbs aren't a recording device. The thought is already gone by the time I'm fighting autocorrect.

What works: press record, keep walking, keep thinking. Here's the pipeline I built to make voice memos actually land somewhere I'll find them again.

## The problem that kept happening

Ideas were disappearing between "I just had a thought" and getting back to the laptop. The cost wasn't the frustration — I could live with that. The cost was everything downstream: posts I never wrote, decisions I re-litigated from scratch, problems I'd already solved and then re-solved.

The ideas weren't bad. They just weren't durable.

## What the capture looks like now

Phone comes out. I press record in the Voice Memos app. I keep walking and keep talking.

A recent one, from April 5, started like this:

> Okay. So I'm outside. I'm gonna test doing voice recordings instead of writing it out as I walk. 'Cause, yeah, I think this is valuable time. I capture a lot of really good ideas, and I wanna have this time…

Eight minutes and thirty-eight seconds of thinking-out-loud, most of which I'd have lost typing.

Voice-first matters because thinking-on-the-move has a different shape than thinking-at-the-desk. You follow a thought further before deciding whether it's good. You catch yourself mid-sentence — "I don't like saying X because it's too vague" — in a way that typing suppresses. The memo preserves the texture of actual reasoning, not just the conclusion.

## The pipeline, step by step

When I'm done recording, I hit share in the Voice Memos app and pick a Shortcut I built called **Transcribe**. That's the whole interaction on the phone — no app to open, no file to rename, no copy-paste.

The Shortcut POSTs the audio file to a Cloudflare Workers endpoint. The URL is public, but the Worker is gated behind a Cloudflare Access service token, so only requests carrying my token get through. Public in the sense that it's reachable from anywhere; private in the sense that only I can call it. I get Cloudflare's edge protections around what is effectively a private endpoint.

From there, the Worker hands the job off to an [Inngest](https://www.inngest.com/) pipeline. Inngest does the async heavy lifting: calling [Eleven Labs](https://elevenlabs.io/)'s speech-to-text API and writing the result through to my Obsidian vault's `sources/` folder. I picked Eleven Labs over Whisper and Otter because the transcription quality on natural conversation — stammers, self-corrections, trailing-off sentences — was meaningfully better in my testing. Otter is built for meetings; a voice memo where I'm interrupting myself every few sentences isn't a meeting.

The final write looks like this — a markdown file with frontmatter that makes it searchable and linkable:

```yaml
---
categories:
  - "[[Sources]]"
source_type: voice-memo
file_path: "/Users/kyrelldixon/.kos/agent/captures/voice-memo-1775438033884/audio.m4a"
created: "[[2026-04-05]]"
duration: "8:38"
extraction_method: elevenlabs
status: raw
---
```

That frontmatter is the part that makes this more than a dump. `categories` links it to the Sources hub note. `created` is a wikilinked date, so the memo shows up on the daily note for April 5 automatically. `source_type: voice-memo` lets me filter voice memos separately from articles, tweets, videos. `status: raw` flags it as untouched — I know I haven't extracted anything from it yet.

## What the output looks like

Here's a real excerpt from that April 5 memo, unedited, straight out of the pipeline:

> So really, I think for me, the, the challenge is content strategy. It's not what can I post. It's what, what can I post that gets me the best results in the least amount of time? 'Cause that's what's most valuable to me. Best results, least amount of time.

That line — *best results, least amount of time* — turned out to be the sharpest articulation of what I actually want from a publishing habit. I'd have never surfaced it at a keyboard. Walking, it showed up buried a few minutes in, after I'd already rambled past three or four other thoughts.

The transcript preserves everything: the stumbles, the stammers, the self-correction. That's what makes it usable later. I skim for the moments where my own voice gets sharp — those are usually the ideas worth pulling into a draft.

## What I do with it after

The capture isn't the value. What I do with it is.

- **Search.** Obsidian full-text search across `sources/` surfaces the memo when I'm drafting something months later and vaguely remember "didn't I think about this on a walk?"
- **Link.** When I write a post, I wikilink to the memo as a source — readers can't see it, but I can trace where an idea came from.
- **Pull quotes.** A sharp line (like *best results, least amount of time*) is already citable — no re-typing, no paraphrasing.

This beats a generic notes-app dump because the memo isn't orphaned. It's part of a graph — linked to a date, categorized with other sources, searchable alongside everything else I've captured.

## Building it with Claude Code

Most of this pipeline — the Shortcut, the Worker, the Inngest function, the write-to-vault step — was built with Claude Code. Most of it was wiring: HTTP handlers, API calls, file writes. The parts I had to steer on — picking Eleven Labs over other STT providers, the frontmatter schema so it played well with my existing vault conventions, the Cloudflare Access service-token model — were the parts that needed my judgment. The scaffolding was routine.

That split shows up everywhere in AI work: the parts that need you, the parts that don't. Naming which is which is most of the skill.

## Rough edges

- **Nothing downstream is automatic yet.** The memo lands in `sources/`, but no agent reads it and suggests extractions. `status: raw` just sits there until I manually change it. Next iteration: an agent that reads raw memos and proposes topic tags + candidate pulls.
